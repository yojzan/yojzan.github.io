We Are Alone Together and Nobody Cares

I don't know when it happened. Maybe it was always like this and I just never noticed. Or maybe something broke somewhere along the way, quietly, without any announcement, and we all just kept scrolling.

People are lonely. Not the romantic kind. Not the I miss someone kind. The deep kind. The nobody would notice if I disappeared kind. And somehow we turned that into a political problem to be managed.

Governments talk about social cohesion. Researchers publish papers. Therapists are booked six weeks out. And the loneliness just grows.

Here's what nobody wants to say. We built this. Not accidentally. Deliberately. Every system around you, the city design, the work culture, the phone in your hand, was optimized to keep you just connected enough to not revolt, and just isolated enough to stay consuming.

You don't have a community. You have a feed.

And the terrifying part? People are filling that void. With ideology. With rage. With movements that give them belonging at the cost of their mind. Radicalization isn't about bad ideas reaching good people. It's about empty people reaching for anything warm.

The far right understood this before anyone else did. So did cults. So did every demagogue in history. Lonely people are not dangerous because they're angry. They're dangerous because they're desperate to belong.

And the left? Still talking about policy. Still writing reports. Still missing the point entirely.

Nobody is coming to fix this. Your neighbor doesn't know your name. Your city wasn't built for you to exist in, it was built for you to move through, spend money and leave. Your phone gives you the sensation of connection without any of its weight.

And you sit there watching other people's lives feeling vaguely like you're failing at something nobody taught you.

You are not failing. You were failed.
