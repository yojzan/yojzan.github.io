The Boulder Was Never Yours

I used to think the hardest part of being human was the suffering. I was wrong. The hardest part is that we suffer for things that were never ours to carry in the first place.

Camus gave us Sisyphus. A man condemned to push a boulder up a hill for eternity. Every time he reaches the top it rolls back down. And Camus, in what I think is either the most optimistic or the most cruel line in all of philosophy, tells us we must imagine Sisyphus happy.

I spent a long time thinking about that happiness. What does it look like. Where does it live. Is it the grim satisfaction of a man who has made peace with his punishment. Is it defiance. Is it something closer to numbness dressed up in noble clothing.

But recently I started thinking about a different question entirely.

What if Sisyphus stopped.

Not because he gave up. Not because the boulder crushed him finally, not because despair won. But because one ordinary day, halfway up that hill, arms burning, breath short, he looked at the boulder and thought — wait. When did I agree to this. Whose boulder is this actually.

And the answer, when it came, was quiet and devastating.

It was never his.

We do not talk enough about inherited weight. The things we carry that were handed to us so early, so seamlessly, that we genuinely cannot remember a time before them. The expectations that arrived before we could consent to them. The definitions of success that were already written before we were born. The version of yourself your family needed you to be. The version your country needed. Your religion. Your gender. Your neighborhood.

You picked all of it up. You started walking. You called it life.

And when it got heavy, when your back bent and your knees shook, you did not question the boulder. You questioned yourself. Your weakness. Your lack of discipline. Your failure to push harder.

That is the real punishment. Not the boulder. The belief that the boulder belongs to you.

Camus was writing about meaning. About a universe that does not care, that offers no explanation, no reward, no narrative arc where the good guy wins and the suffering was worth it. He called this the absurd. The collision between human beings desperately wanting meaning and a universe that offers nothing but silence in return.

His answer was rebellion. Keep pushing. Embrace the struggle. Find your meaning in the act itself not the outcome.

I respect that answer. I lived that answer for years.

But I think there is something Camus left unfinished.

Because not every boulder is the universe's indifference. Some boulders are just other people's expectations. Some are systems. Some are economic structures that needed you exhausted and too busy to ask questions. Some are cultural scripts handed down through generations like heirlooms nobody actually wants but nobody knows how to throw away.

And those boulders do not require rebellion. They require something quieter and in some ways harder.

They require you to put them down.

I know a man, I will not say where or who, who spent fifteen years climbing toward a version of success that made him feel nothing. Not unhappy exactly. Just hollow. He had the apartment. The title. The respect of people whose respect he had decided mattered. He pushed the boulder. Every single day he pushed it.

And one morning he woke up, looked at the ceiling, and the only thought in his head was this is not mine.

Not the apartment. Not the title. The entire shape of the life. The whole architecture of it. Built from other people's ideas about what a life should look like.

He did not have a dramatic breakdown. He did not quit everything and move to the mountains. He just got very quiet for a while. And in that quiet he started asking, for maybe the first time honestly, what do I actually want. Not what should I want. Not what would make people proud. What do I want.

The answers were small and almost embarrassing in their simplicity. He wanted to cook. He wanted to walk without a destination. He wanted to read books nobody had assigned him. He wanted conversations that went nowhere useful and lasted until the restaurant closed.

He wanted, in short, to exist without justifying the existence.

That is not a midlife crisis. That is a man finally looking at his boulder.

Here is what I think happens to most of us.

We grow up inside systems, families, cultures, economies, that have a very specific idea of what a good human being looks like. Productive. Achieving. Moving forward. Accumulating. The boulder has a shape and that shape is called progress and we are told that pushing it is the whole point.

And we push. God we push. We push through exhaustion and doubt and that quiet voice at three in the morning that asks if any of this is real. We push because stopping feels like failure and failure feels like death and so we keep going and we call it resilience and we get praised for it.

But resilience toward what. In service of what. Whose definition of the summit are we climbing toward.

These are not comfortable questions. They do not have clean answers. And asking them out loud, in a world that rewards pushing, feels almost transgressive. Like you are breaking a rule nobody wrote down but everyone enforces.

Camus said we must imagine Sisyphus happy because the alternative, despair, is surrender to the absurd. If you stop pushing you are admitting the universe won.

But I want to offer a different reading.

What if stopping is not surrender. What if it is the first genuinely free act available to you.

Not stopping forever. Not abandoning effort or meaning or engagement with life. But stopping long enough to look at what you are carrying. To hold it at arm's length and ask with real honesty, is this mine. Did I choose this. Would I choose this if I were choosing freely right now, today, knowing what I know.

Some of it you will pick back up. Some boulders are genuinely yours, chosen, meaningful, worth the burning arms and the shaking knees. The work you actually love. The people you actually chose. The values that came from inside you not from fear of what happens if you deviate.

Those boulders are different. They are heavy in a way that feels like aliveness rather than punishment.

But some of it you will leave on the hill. And walking away from it will feel terrifying for exactly one reason. Because you will have to figure out who you are without it.

And that, I think, is the real absurd. Not a universe without meaning. But a self without a borrowed identity. Standing there on the hill, hands empty, finally free, completely lost, having to build something real from scratch.

Sisyphus stopped. Not from despair.

From finally understanding the boulder was never his.

And for the first time in eternity he looked out from the hill, not at the summit, not at the boulder waiting at the bottom, but at the horizon. Wide and indifferent and completely open.

And what he felt in that moment was not happiness exactly.

It was something older and stranger and more honest than happiness.

It was the terrifying, necessary feeling of beginning.
